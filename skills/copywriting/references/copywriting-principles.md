# Web Copywriting Principles

Shared knowledge base for the Web Copywriter agent. Operational rules distilled from Ogilvy, Wiebe (Copyhackers), Handley (Everybody Writes), Clark (Copyblogger), and 52-agent research swarm.

---

## 1. Copy Formulas Reference

### PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve)
1. State the problem in the customer's words
2. Intensify the emotional consequence of inaction
3. Present the mechanism of relief

Best for: short landing pages, email, ad copy, pain-driven products.

### AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)
1. Hook with the most compelling claim
2. Build curiosity with specifics and mechanism
3. Paint the transformed state
4. Single, clear CTA

Best for: longer-form pages, product launches, narrative journeys.

### BAB (Before-After-Bridge)
1. Their current painful state
2. Their desired state achieved
3. Your product is the bridge

Best for: transformation narratives, DTC, fitness, coaching, visual media.

### The 4 A's (Clark)
1. **Attention** — Magnetic headline (50% of writing time here)
2. **Attraction** — Address specific pain so the right reader self-selects
3. **Authority** — Prove expertise by solving a small problem
4. **Action** — Single, clear, logical CTA

### StoryBrand One-Liner
**Who you help + What you do + Specific benefit.** One sentence. No jargon.

## 2. Headline Formulas

| Formula | Example |
|---|---|
| Problem-Solution | "Stop drowning in spreadsheets. Start making decisions." |
| How-To Without | "How to Close Your Books in Hours Without Hiring an Accountant" |
| Number + Adjective | "The 7 Underrated Ways to Reduce Cloud Costs" |
| Question | "Do You Recognize the 5 Warning Signs of Data Drift?" |
| Testimonial | "'We cut deployment time by 80%' — CTO, Acme Corp" |
| Negative | "Why Your Landing Page Isn't Converting (And the 3 Fixes)" |

Rules: 6-12 words. Primary keyword early. Benefit, not feature. Never puns unless the brand is explicitly playful.

## 3. The Handley Operational Rules

### Formula of Three
Content must have all three or it fails:
- **Utility** — solves a real problem
- **Inspiration** — motivates action or new thinking
- **Empathy** — written from the reader's perspective, not the company's

### The Ugly First Draft (TUFD)
Separate generation from editing. Write badly first. Edit for the reader second. "The real writing happens during revision."

### Rules of Economy
- No ten-dollar words when one-dollar words work: help > facilitate, use > utilize
- Max 3 sentences per paragraph, 6 lines max
- Subhead every 300 words (benefit-oriented, not label-oriented)
- Lede (first sentence) hooks. Kicker (last sentence) resolves or calls to action.
- "No one will ever complain that you've made things too simple to understand."

### The Squawk Test
Cover the logo. Could a competitor have written this? If yes, rewrite.
- Define 3 personality adjectives with clarifiers
- Ban corporate cartilage: "leverage," "synergy," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge"
- Use "you" and "we," use contractions
- Vary sentence length deliberately — rhythm is voice

### The So What? Filter
Apply to every paragraph. If the answer is about the company, ask again. Stop when the answer is entirely about the customer's life.

## 4. Clark's Authority Rules

### Rule of One
Every piece of content: one reader, one big idea, one offer. Multiple CTAs = analysis paralysis.

### Teaching as Selling
Give away the "how" (method) to sell the "what" (product). Teaching establishes trust. Demonstrate expertise by solving a small problem for free.

### Magnetic Headlines
Spend 50% of writing time on the headline. 8/10 read the headline; 2/10 read the body. The headline is the ad for the content.

## 5. UX Writing Patterns

### Error Messages
**Problem + Solution path. Never blame.**
| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "Error 400" | "That email doesn't look right — check for typos" |
| "Invalid input" | "Please enter a number between 1 and 100" |
| "Something went wrong" | "We couldn't save your changes. Try again?" |

### Empty States
**Positive title + why empty + single CTA.**
| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "No data found." | "No projects yet. Create your first one." |
| "Nothing here." | "You're all caught up! Check back later." |

### Button Labels
**Action verb + outcome. Sentence case. 1-3 words.**
| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "Submit" | "Send message" |
| "Click here" | "Download report" |
| "Yes / No" | "Keep editing / Discard changes" |

### Success States (Mentor Voice)
- Celebrate the Hero: "Your campaign is live!" not "Campaign saved."
- Show next step: "Share with your team?" not just confirmation

### Onboarding
- Max 3-5 steps. Active voice. Benefit hint per step.
- Welcome message acknowledges why they're here
- Skip option always available (power users)

## 6. Number Presentation Rules

| Rule | Bad | Good |
|---|---|---|
| Specific > abstract | "nearly half" | "47%" |
| Relative > absolute | "loads in 1.2s" | "3x faster than competitors" |
| Personal > aggregate | "Users save $12K avg" | "You could save $14,000/year" |
| Context > raw | "500GB storage" | "Enough for 100,000 photos" |
| Time > money (for B2C) | "Save $20/month" | "Get back 5 hours every week" |

## 7. Voice & Tone Matrix

| Context | Tone Shift | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding | Warm, encouraging | "Welcome! Let's get you set up." |
| Error | Calm, helpful | "Something didn't work. Here's what to try." |
| Success | Celebratory | "You did it! Your first report is ready." |
| Pricing | Confident, clear | "Simple pricing. No surprises." |
| Security | Serious, trustworthy | "Your data is encrypted end-to-end." |
| Empty state | Optimistic, guiding | "Nothing here yet. That's about to change." |

The four dimensions (Mailchimp model): Formality, Humor, Respect, Enthusiasm — each slides independently based on context.

## 8. Reading Behavior Patterns

- **F-pattern**: horizontal sweep at top, shorter sweep mid, vertical scan left edge
- **Layer-cake**: eyes fixate on headings, drop to relevant body text
- **Z-pattern**: best for CTA-focused, low-density pages
- Users read only 28% of text on average — structure for scanning
- Bold, subheads, and bullets outperform paragraphs for discoverability
- Mobile forces earlier vertical scanning — tighter copy essential

## 9. Accessibility in Copy

- WCAG 3.1.5 targets lower secondary reading level (8th grade Flesch-Kincaid)
- Max 25 words per sentence, 5 sentences per paragraph
- Avoid cultural metaphors that exclude non-native speakers
- Person-first language ("people with disabilities")
- Alt text = context-driven narrative, not just visual description
- Never rely on color alone to convey meaning
